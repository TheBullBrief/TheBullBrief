What You Need to Know About the Three Financial Statements?

By Vinay Yerramsetti | September 18, 2024

The three core financial statements—the Income Statement, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow Statement—are fundamental tools for understanding a company’s financial health. Investment bankers rely heavily on these statements to assess a company’s profitability, financial stability, and cash flow, which are essential for valuations, mergers and acquisitions, and capital raising.

The Income Statement, also referred to as the Profit & Loss (P&L) statement, measures a company’s financial performance over a specific period, such as a quarter or fiscal year. It starts with Revenue, which represents the total income from the sale of goods or services. From this, Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)—the direct costs associated with producing goods or services—is subtracted to calculate Gross Profit, a critical indicator of the company’s efficiency in production or service delivery. Next, Operating Expenses, such as salaries, rent, and R&D costs, are deducted to arrive at Operating Income (EBIT), or Earnings Before Interest and Taxes. EBIT is a key metric in investment banking as it reflects the profitability of core operations before factoring in capital structure and tax effects. After deducting Interest Expense and Taxes, the result is Net Income, which represents the company’s bottom-line profitability. This metric is crucial for understanding how much profit remains for equity holders and is often used to calculate ratios like Earnings Per Share (EPS).

The Balance Sheet provides a snapshot of a company’s financial position at a specific point in time, organized into three categories: Assets, Liabilities, and Equity. Assets are divided into Current Assets, such as cash, accounts receivable, and inventory, and Non-Current Assets, including property, plant, equipment (PPE), and intangible assets like goodwill. Liabilities are similarly split into Current Liabilities, like accounts payable and accrued expenses, and Non-Current Liabilities, such as long-term debt. Equity, the residual value for shareholders, includes items like retained earnings and common stock. Investment bankers use the Assets = Liabilities + Equity equation to ensure the Balance Sheet is balanced. Additionally, metrics derived from the Balance Sheet, such as the Current Ratio (Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities) and Debt-to-Equity Ratio, are key to assessing liquidity, leverage, and financial risk—critical factors when structuring deals or evaluating a company’s creditworthiness.

The Cash Flow Statement details the cash inflows and outflows over a specific period and is divided into three sections: Operating Activities, Investing Activities, and Financing Activities. The Operating Activities section starts with Net Income from the Income Statement and adjusts for non-cash items like depreciation and changes in working capital, providing insight into the company’s ability to generate cash from its core operations. This section is particularly important for investment bankers as it reflects the sustainability of earnings and the efficiency of working capital management. The Investing Activities section includes cash flows related to the purchase or sale of assets, such as capital expenditures (CapEx) and acquisitions. High levels of CapEx can indicate growth potential, while divestitures may signal strategic repositioning. The Financing Activities section captures cash flows from debt issuance or repayment, equity financing, and dividend payments, providing insight into the company’s capital structure. The Net Change in Cash from these three sections reconciles the starting and ending cash balances on the Balance Sheet.

Investment bankers pay close attention to how these three statements interconnect. For instance, Net Income from the Income Statement feeds into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet and serves as the starting point for Operating Cash Flow on the Cash Flow Statement. Changes in working capital, reflected in the Balance Sheet, are incorporated into the Cash Flow Statement, showing how efficiently a company manages its short-term obligations. Furthermore, the ending cash balance on the Cash Flow Statement directly aligns with the cash account under Current Assets on the Balance Sheet. These linkages are crucial when constructing financial models, such as a three-statement model, which investment bankers use for forecasting future performance, valuation, and scenario analysis.

For investment banking, the most critical points include analyzing EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) from the Income Statement as a proxy for operating cash flow, understanding leverage ratios from the Balance Sheet to assess risk, and scrutinizing cash flow from operations to gauge liquidity and sustainability. Additionally, the quality of earnings, working capital efficiency, and capital structure—all derived from these statements—play vital roles in deal-making, valuation, and due diligence processes. Mastery of these statements and their interplay is essential for excelling in investment banking and driving data-driven decisions in complex transactions.
